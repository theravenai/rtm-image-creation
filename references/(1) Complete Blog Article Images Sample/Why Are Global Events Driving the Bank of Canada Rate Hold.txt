<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-the-bank-of-canada-rate-hold-and-what-it-means-for-your-spring-plans"><strong>The Bank Of Canada Rate Hold And What It Means For Your Spring Plans</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The Bank of Canada<span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"> </span><a href="https://www.bankofcanada.ca/2026/03/fad-press-release-2026-03-18/&amp;sa=D&amp;source=editors&amp;ust=1773942769162248&amp;usg=AOvVaw0eTmyjPrFvYlwxu5Yav1Rx"> held its policy rate at 2.25%</a>. No surprise there.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>This is not relief. <strong>It is a pause.</strong> Too many Canadians are going to misread this as good news when, in reality, <strong>the pressure has not gone away.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>There is still too much uncertainty in the world right now.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Tension in the Middle East is pushing oil prices up, and that makes everyday costs rise again. At the same time, <strong>inflation in the US </strong><span style="box-sizing: border-box; margin: 0px; padding: 0px;"><strong>re</strong></span><strong>mains high, keeping borrowing costs elevated in Canada.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The Bank of Canada didn’t lower or increase rates because <strong>the outlook is still too unstable.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>So where does that leave you as a homeowner or buyer heading into spring?</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-are-global-events-actually-affecting-mortgage-rates-right-now"><strong>How are global events actually affecting mortgage rates right now?</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>We are seeing <strong>ongoing geopolitical tension in the Middle East,</strong> particularly involving oil-producing regions. When that heats up, oil prices rise.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>When oil rises, inflation follows. It hits fuel, transportation, groceries, and pretty much every part of the economy.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>At the same time, the US is still dealing with stubborn inflation. </strong>If the Federal Reserve keeps rates higher for longer, that pushes bond yields up globally, including here in Canada.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>When bond yields rise, <strong>fixed mortgage rates follow.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Then you layer in trade uncertainty and tariffs, <strong>which increase the cost of goods and slow down global growth.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>That creates a messy situation where <strong>inflation can stay elevated even as the economy softens.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>So while this rate hold looks calm, <strong>the risks underneath are not.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What factors are affecting mortgage rates?</strong></h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Oil price volatility from geopolitical conflict can quickly push inflation higher.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>US inflation and Federal Reserve policy are keeping global bond yields elevated.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Rising bond yields directly increase fixed mortgage rates in Canada.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Trade tensions and tariffs are adding cost pressure across supply chains.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Inflation can reaccelerate even in a slowing economy.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>The Bank of Canada will prioritize inflation control over rate cuts.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-did-the-bank-of-canada-just-signal-lower-rates-are-coming"><strong>Did the Bank of Canada just signal lower rates are coming?</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>No. <strong>A hold is not a signal that cuts are around the corner.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>What it tells me is <strong>the Bank is not confident enough to move in either direction.</strong> Inflation is behaving, but not completely under control.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>The economy is slowing, but not enough to justify stimulus.</strong></p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-what-happens-to-your-mortgage-right-now"><strong>What happens to your mortgage right now?</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Simple answer, <strong>not much changes today.</strong> </p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>If you have a variable rate, you get stability for now. <strong>Your rate and payment likely stay where they are.</strong></p>

<p>If you are looking for a fixed rate or<strong> <a href="https://askross.ca/10-ways-to-manage-higher-mortgage-payments-in-2026/">renewing soon</a>, </strong>this is where it can hurt. <strong>Fixed rates have already been creeping up,</strong> and this announcement does nothing to stop that.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-why-are-fixed-rates-still-going-up"><strong>Why are fixed rates still going up?</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Because the Bank of Canada <strong>does not control fixed rates.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p><strong>Bond yields do. Right now, <a href="https://askross.ca/mortgage-renewal-rates-forecast-2026-canada/">bond yields are rising</a> </strong>because of inflation concerns, oil prices, and global uncertainty.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>This is where I see the most confusion. People hear “rates held” and assume everything freezes. <strong>It does not.</strong></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What is actually driving fixed rates</strong></h3>

<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Oil prices are pushing inflation risk.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Global instability is keeping markets on edge.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Lenders are pricing in future uncertainty.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Fixed mortgage pricing is reacting to all of this, not the Bank alone.</li>

<p>If your mortgage is coming up for <a href="https://askross.ca/what-will-happen-to-my-mortgage-payments-when-my-low-rate-expires/">renewal soon, </a><strong>do not sit around waiting for the Bank of Canada to lower rates.</strong></p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-list-of-all-faqs-bank-of-canada-rate-hold-and-mortgage-impact"><strong>List of all FAQs: Bank of Canada Rate Hold and Mortgage Impact</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>So, why did the Bank of Canada hold its rate?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>The Bank is waiting due to ongoing inflation risks and global uncertainty.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>It reflects caution, not confidence in lowering or raising rates.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How are global events actually affecting mortgage rates right now?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Oil prices, US inflation, and global tensions are pushing inflation and bond yields higher.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Higher bond yields are directly increasing fixed mortgage rates.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Did the Bank of Canada signal rate cuts are coming?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>No, a hold does not guarantee future rate cuts.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>It indicates uncertainty and a wait-and-see approach.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Could rates still go higher later this year?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Yes, especially if inflation rises again or global risks worsen.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>External factors remain the biggest threat.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What happens to your mortgage right now?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Variable rates stay stable, but fixed rates can still increase.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Renewals may still come at higher costs.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Why are fixed rates still going up?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Fixed rates follow bond yields, not the Bank of Canada directly.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Rising inflation concerns and global instability are pushing yields higher.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Should you renew now or wait?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Waiting is risky if rates rise further.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Early planning and rate comparison are key to making the right decision.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What does this mean for spring home buyers?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>The market is balanced with more inventory and less competition.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Buyers have more negotiating power but still face affordability challenges.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Are prices dropping in Toronto and Vancouver?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Prices are mostly flat or slightly soft, especially in condo markets.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>No major crash is currently happening.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What should you expect from the spring market overall?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>A slower, more balanced market with cautious buyers.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Long term supply issues may develop due to reduced construction.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>What should Canadians do right now?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Focus on budgeting, planning, and reducing debt.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Make decisions based on current rates, not future predictions.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How do interest rates affect mortgage payments in Canada?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Higher rates increase monthly payments and reduce borrowing power.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Lower rates improve affordability but are not guaranteed soon.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Is it better to choose fixed or variable rates right now?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Fixed offers stability and predictability.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Variable may offer flexibility, but comes with risk if rates rise.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading" id="h-how-can-i-prepare-for-a-mortgage-renewal-in-a-high-rate-environment"><strong>How can I prepare for a mortgage renewal in a high-rate environment?</strong></h4>

<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Start early, review your finances, and explore options.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Work with a professional to compare lenders and strategies.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Will mortgage rates go down in 2026?</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>There is no certainty; rates depend on inflation and global conditions.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Planning should assume rates may stay higher longer.</li>
